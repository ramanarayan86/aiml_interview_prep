# Q4‑05 · What's the difference between on-policy and off-policy RL? Which category does PPO fall into?

<div align="center">

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The 20-second answer.** **On-policy** algorithms use only data collected by the *current* policy to compute each gradient update, then discard that data. **Off-policy** algorithms store experience in a replay buffer and can reuse data collected by *older* policies, correcting for the distribution shift with importance weights. **PPO is classified as on-policy**: it collects rollouts from the current policy $\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}$, applies the clipped surrogate objective over those rollouts for a small number of mini-epochs (typically 4–16), then discards the batch entirely. The clipping ratio $\varepsilon$ (usually 0.2) is what keeps the within-batch reuse safe — it prevents the updated policy from straying too far from $\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}$. PPO is the dominant choice in RLHF because it is stable, clip-controlled, and integrates cleanly with a KL penalty on the reward signal.

## 1 · First principles: what does "policy" mean in RL?

A **policy** $\pi_\theta(a \mid s)$ is a probability distribution over actions $a$ given state $s$, parameterised by $\theta$ (neural network weights in deep RL). The RL objective is to find $\theta$ that maximises expected cumulative reward:

$$J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_\theta}\left[\sum_{t=0}^{T} \gamma^t r_t\right]$$

where $\tau$ is a trajectory (sequence of states, actions, rewards), and $\gamma \in [0,1]$ is the discount factor.

The policy gradient theorem (Williams, 1992; Sutton et al., 1999) gives us the gradient of $J$ with respect to $\theta$:

$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(s_t, a_t) \sim \pi_\theta}\left[\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t) \cdot \hat{A}_t\right]$$

where $\hat{A}_t$ is the **advantage** — how much better action $a_t$ was than the average action the policy would have taken in state $s_t$.

The critical detail in this formula: the expectation is over $(s_t, a_t)$ drawn from **the current policy** $\pi_\theta$. This is the root of the on-policy/off-policy distinction.

---

## 2 · On-policy learning — definition and intuition

**Definition.** An algorithm is **on-policy** if every gradient update uses data sampled from the *current version* of the policy being updated.

The on-policy loop is:

1. Roll out the current policy $\pi_\theta$ in the environment to collect a batch of trajectories.
2. Estimate advantages $\hat{A}_t$ from those trajectories.
3. Compute and apply the policy gradient update — changing $\theta \to \theta'$.
4. **Discard all collected data.** It was generated by $\pi_\theta$, which no longer exists (the policy is now $\pi_{\theta'}$).
5. Go to step 1 with the new policy.

**Why discard?** Because the policy gradient estimator requires the data distribution to match the policy distribution. If we kept old data from $\pi_\theta$ and used it to update $\pi_{\theta'}$, the gradient estimate would be biased — the data would over-represent states and actions that $\pi_{\theta'}$ visits less frequently.

**Sample efficiency cost.** Generating rollouts (especially in environments like LLM text generation or robotics simulators) is expensive. On-policy algorithms discard all that data after one update, making them **sample-inefficient** by construction.

**Stability benefit.** Because the data always reflects the current policy, the gradient estimates are unbiased. There is no "distributional mismatch" problem. The algorithm converges more predictably.

**Classic on-policy algorithms:**
- REINFORCE (vanilla policy gradient)
- A3C / A2C (Asynchronous/Synchronous Advantage Actor-Critic)
- TRPO (Trust Region Policy Optimization — the PPO predecessor)
- PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization — the dominant RLHF algorithm)

## 4 · Off-policy learning — definition and intuition

**Definition.** An algorithm is **off-policy** if gradient updates can use data collected by a policy *different* from the one being updated. The data source policy is called the **behavior policy** $\pi_b$; the policy being improved is the **target policy** $\pi_\theta$.

The core mechanism: **importance sampling**. The policy gradient estimator normally requires samples from $\pi_\theta$, but if samples come from $\pi_b$, we can reweight them:

$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(s_t, a_t) \sim \pi_b}\left[\frac{\pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)}{\pi_b(a_t \mid s_t)} \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t) \cdot \hat{A}_t\right]$$

The ratio $\rho_t = \pi_\theta / \pi_b$ is the **importance weight** — it corrects for the fact that $\pi_b$ visits some state-action pairs more (or less) frequently than $\pi_\theta$ would.

**The replay buffer.** Off-policy algorithms store transitions $(s_t, a_t, r_t, s_{t+1})$ in a large buffer. Each update samples a random mini-batch from the buffer, which may contain transitions generated by many past versions of the policy. This allows:
- **Reuse of data** — one transition can contribute to thousands of updates.
- **Decorrelation** — random sampling from the buffer breaks temporal correlation between consecutive updates.
- **Asynchronous collection** — actors can collect data in parallel while a learner processes the buffer.

**Sample efficiency.** Off-policy algorithms typically require far fewer environment interactions to reach a given performance level. DQN (Mnih et al., 2015) famously used a 1M-transition replay buffer, allowing each environment step to be reused in roughly 50 gradient updates.

**The catch: distribution mismatch.** When $\pi_\theta$ diverges substantially from $\pi_b$, importance weights become large (or small), causing high variance or biased gradient estimates. This is why off-policy training can be less stable than on-policy training when the policy changes rapidly.

**Classic off-policy algorithms:**
- DQN (Deep Q-Network) — Q-value learning with replay buffer
- DDPG (Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient)
- SAC (Soft Actor-Critic) — entropy-regularised, state of the art for continuous control
- TD3 (Twin Delayed DDPG)

## 6 · The PPO algorithm — where it sits in the taxonomy

### Is PPO on-policy or off-policy?

**PPO is on-policy**, with a carefully engineered approximation that allows limited data reuse within a single rollout batch.

Here is the exact PPO update loop:

1. **Collect rollouts.** Run the current policy $\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}$ for $T$ timesteps to collect a batch of $(s_t, a_t, r_t, \hat{A}_t)$ tuples. Record the log-probabilities $\log \pi_{\theta_\text{old}}(a_t \mid s_t)$.

2. **Compute advantages.** Estimate $\hat{A}_t$ using GAE (Generalized Advantage Estimation) or a simpler returns-minus-baseline estimator.

3. **Mini-epoch updates.** For $K = 4\text{–}16$ mini-epochs, shuffle the batch into mini-batches and compute gradient updates using the **clipped surrogate objective**:

$$\mathcal{L}^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t \left[ \min\left( r_t(\theta)\, \hat{A}_t,\; \text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\varepsilon, 1+\varepsilon)\, \hat{A}_t \right) \right]$$

where the probability ratio is:

$$r_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}(a_t \mid s_t)}$$

4. **Discard the batch.** After all $K$ mini-epochs, the collected data is discarded. The updated policy becomes $\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}$ for the next rollout.

### Why the nuance?

The $K$ mini-epochs mean PPO technically reuses the same batch for multiple gradient steps, making it *slightly* off-policy within that batch. The probability ratio $r_t(\theta)$ measures exactly how much the policy has moved from the batch's generating distribution $\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}$.

The clipping at $1 \pm \varepsilon$ is what keeps this safe: if the policy strays outside the trust region, the gradient becomes zero — the clip eliminates the incentive to move further. This is the key insight of PPO over its predecessor TRPO (which used a hard KL constraint instead of clipping).

The RL literature classifies PPO as **on-policy** because:
- Data is collected from the current (or very recent) policy, not a replay buffer.
- The data is discarded after a small number of updates.
- There is no long-lived replay buffer accumulating transitions from diverse past policies.

### Comparison table

| Property | On-Policy (PPO) | Off-Policy (SAC/DQN) |
|---|---|---|
| Data source | Current policy $\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}$ | Replay buffer (all past policies) |
| Data reuse | Within-batch only ($K$ mini-epochs) | Unlimited (full replay buffer) |
| Importance weighting | Implicit via clip ratio $r_t(\theta)$ | Explicit $\pi_\theta / \pi_b$ correction |
| Sample efficiency | Lower | Higher |
| Stability | Higher (no old data divergence) | Lower (needs careful tuning) |
| RLHF suitability | Excellent (standard choice) | Less common; ILQL is one approach |

## 7 · PPO PyTorch reference implementation

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F

def ppo_loss(
    log_probs_new: torch.Tensor,   # log pi_theta(a_t | s_t), shape [B]
    log_probs_old: torch.Tensor,   # log pi_theta_old(a_t | s_t), shape [B]
    advantages: torch.Tensor,      # hat{A}_t, shape [B]
    clip_eps: float = 0.2,
    entropy_coef: float = 0.01,
    entropy: torch.Tensor = None,  # optional entropy bonus, shape [B]
) -> torch.Tensor:
    """
    Computes the PPO clipped surrogate loss.

    Returns a scalar loss (to be minimised; note: gradient *ascent*
    on J means gradient *descent* on -L^CLIP).
    """
    # Probability ratio r_t(theta) = pi_theta / pi_theta_old
    # Computed in log-space for numerical stability
    ratio = torch.exp(log_probs_new - log_probs_old)   # shape [B]

    # Clipped surrogate objective (equation 7 in Schulman et al. 2017)
    unclipped = ratio * advantages
    clipped   = torch.clamp(ratio, 1.0 - clip_eps, 1.0 + clip_eps) * advantages
    policy_loss = -torch.mean(torch.min(unclipped, clipped))

    # Optional entropy bonus (encourages exploration)
    if entropy is not None:
        policy_loss = policy_loss - entropy_coef * entropy.mean()

    return policy_loss


def ppo_update(
    policy: nn.Module,
    optimizer: torch.optim.Optimizer,
    states: torch.Tensor,       # [B, state_dim]
    actions: torch.Tensor,      # [B] (discrete) or [B, action_dim] (continuous)
    advantages: torch.Tensor,   # [B]
    log_probs_old: torch.Tensor,# [B]  — stored at collection time
    clip_eps: float = 0.2,
    n_epochs: int = 4,
    mini_batch_size: int = 64,
) -> float:
    """
    Run K mini-epochs of PPO updates on the collected batch.
    Returns mean loss for logging.
    """
    B = states.shape[0]
    total_loss = 0.0
    n_updates  = 0

    for _ in range(n_epochs):
        # Shuffle indices for mini-batch construction
        indices = torch.randperm(B)

        for start in range(0, B, mini_batch_size):
            idx = indices[start : start + mini_batch_size]
            mb_states     = states[idx]
            mb_actions    = actions[idx]
            mb_advantages = advantages[idx]
            mb_lp_old     = log_probs_old[idx]

            # Forward pass: get new log-probs under updated policy
            dist = policy(mb_states)               # returns a distribution
            mb_lp_new = dist.log_prob(mb_actions)

            loss = ppo_loss(mb_lp_new, mb_lp_old, mb_advantages, clip_eps)

            optimizer.zero_grad()
            loss.backward()
            # Gradient clipping is standard practice with PPO
            nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(policy.parameters(), max_norm=0.5)
            optimizer.step()

            total_loss += loss.item()
            n_updates  += 1

    return total_loss / n_updates
```

**Key design choices in PPO:**
- `log_probs_old` are computed once at collection time and stored. During mini-epochs, `log_probs_new` drift as $\theta$ updates, making $r_t(\theta) \neq 1$.
- Gradient clipping (`max_norm=0.5`) is standard alongside the PPO clip — two complementary mechanisms preventing large steps.
- Advantages are typically **normalised** per mini-batch (subtract mean, divide by std) to reduce variance and stabilise training.

## 8 · Worked numerical example — the clipping in action

**Setup.** During a PPO mini-epoch, one transition has:
- Probability ratio: $r_t = 1.3$ (policy has become 30% more likely to take this action)
- Advantage: $\hat{A}_t = +1.5$ (this action was 1.5 standard deviations above average)
- Clip parameter: $\varepsilon = 0.2$, so the trust region is $[0.8, 1.2]$

**Step 1: Clip the ratio.**

$$\text{clip}(1.3, 0.8, 1.2) = 1.2$$

The ratio 1.3 exceeds the upper bound $1 + \varepsilon = 1.2$, so it is clamped to 1.2.

**Step 2: Compute both terms.**

$$r_t \cdot \hat{A}_t = 1.3 \times 1.5 = 1.95$$

$$\text{clip}(r_t, 0.8, 1.2) \cdot \hat{A}_t = 1.2 \times 1.5 = 1.80$$

**Step 3: Take the minimum.**

$$\mathcal{L}^{\text{CLIP}} = \min(1.95, 1.80) = 1.80$$

The gradient of 1.80 (with respect to $\theta$) is computed from the **clipped term**, not the unclipped term. The algorithm gets credit for 1.80 but not for the full 1.95 — this removes the gradient signal that would push $r_t$ further above 1.2.

**Why this prevents large steps.** If we used the unclipped objective, the gradient would encourage increasing $\pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)$ further — the loss is 1.95 and higher $r_t$ means higher loss. With clipping, once $r_t > 1.2$ the loss is **flat** in $r_t$ (the gradient is zero with respect to $r_t$). The algorithm cannot gain anything by pushing the policy further outside the trust region.

**Second example: negative advantage (bad action).**

- $r_t = 0.7$, $\hat{A}_t = -1.5$, $\varepsilon = 0.2$ (trust region $[0.8, 1.2]$)
- $\text{clip}(0.7, 0.8, 1.2) = 0.8$
- Unclipped: $0.7 \times (-1.5) = -1.05$
- Clipped: $0.8 \times (-1.5) = -1.20$
- $\min(-1.05, -1.20) = -1.20$

Here the policy has already reduced $r_t$ below the lower bound $1 - \varepsilon = 0.8$ (it's already punishing this action). The clip prevents further over-punishment — the gradient stops pushing $r_t$ below 0.8.

> [!NOTE]
> The clipping is **pessimistic**: for good actions it caps the upside, for bad actions it caps the downside. This is intentional — PPO is conservative by design, preferring smaller, safer updates.

## 9 · Interview drill — follow-up questions

<details>
<summary><b>Q: Why can't we just use the unclipped policy gradient (REINFORCE) with a large learning rate instead of PPO?</b></summary>

REINFORCE with a large learning rate causes **catastrophic policy collapse**: a single bad batch of trajectories can push the policy into a degenerate region (e.g., always outputting one token), after which it collects increasingly bad data, spiraling downward. There is no mechanism to limit the size of gradient steps. TRPO addresses this with a hard KL constraint (expensive to compute via conjugate gradient), and PPO replaces the hard constraint with the cheap clip approximation. The clip acts as a soft trust region — gradient becomes zero outside $[1-\varepsilon, 1+\varepsilon]$, preventing destructive updates.
</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Q: If PPO does K=8 mini-epochs, is it technically off-policy after the first epoch?</b></summary>

Technically yes — after the first gradient step, $\theta \neq \theta_\text{old}$, so subsequent mini-batches from the same rollout are "slightly off-policy." This is why the probability ratio $r_t(\theta)$ is computed: it measures the distributional shift. As $K$ increases, $r_t$ drifts further from 1, and the clip engages more frequently. With $K=4$ and $\varepsilon=0.2$, the drift is small enough that gradient estimates remain approximately unbiased. In practice, $K$ is chosen to stay within the trust region — if the fraction of clipped transitions exceeds ~10%, $K$ is too large. The RL community still classifies PPO as on-policy because the data is discarded after each rollout cycle and no long-lived replay buffer is maintained.
</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Q: Why does RLHF use PPO rather than SAC or DQN?</b></summary>

Three reasons: (1) **Text generation is a discrete, high-dimensional action space** — DQN and DDPG are designed for continuous or small discrete spaces and do not scale to vocabularies of 100K tokens. SAC can handle continuous actions but requires careful entropy tuning. (2) **Stability is paramount** — the LLM policy is a multi-billion parameter network. PPO's clip mechanism prevents catastrophic single-step degradation; off-policy methods with large replay buffers can diverge when the behavior-target policy gap grows. (3) **The KL penalty integrates naturally** — RLHF adds a KL term to the reward signal: $r'_t = r_t - \beta \log(\pi_\theta / \pi_\text{ref})$. This per-step scalar penalty slots directly into PPO's advantage estimation with no architectural changes.
</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Q: What is the relationship between PPO and TRPO?</b></summary>

TRPO (Trust Region Policy Optimization, Schulman et al. 2015) imposes a hard constraint $\mathbb{E}_t[\text{KL}(\pi_{\theta_\text{old}} \| \pi_\theta)] \leq \delta$ using a conjugate gradient solver to find the optimal step within the trust region. This is correct but computationally expensive — it requires computing and inverting a Fisher information matrix approximation, which costs $O(N^2)$ in parameter dimension. PPO replaces the hard KL constraint with the clip heuristic: clip the ratio $r_t$ to $[1-\varepsilon, 1+\varepsilon]$. This eliminates the conjugate gradient step, making PPO vastly simpler to implement and faster to run, while achieving comparable or better empirical performance. Schulman et al. (2017) showed PPO outperforms TRPO on most MuJoCo and Atari benchmarks.
</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Q: Can off-policy algorithms work for RLHF? What is ILQL?</b></summary>

Yes — **Implicit Language Q-Learning (ILQL)** adapts the conservative Q-learning (IQL) framework to language models. It learns a Q-function (token-level value) and a V-function from offline preference data, then extracts a policy via advantage-weighted regression without any environment interaction. This is useful when you have a fixed dataset of completions with rewards (e.g., rated by a trained reward model) and cannot afford online rollouts. The tradeoff: ILQL is more sample-efficient but harder to tune and tends to underperform PPO when online data collection is feasible. For RLHF in practice, the cost of generating rollouts (a forward pass through the LLM) is manageable, so PPO's on-policy stability advantages dominate.
</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Q: What happens to PPO when epsilon is set to 0 or to infinity?</b></summary>

$\varepsilon = 0$ makes the clip immediately active for any policy change: $r_t$ is clamped to exactly 1.0, so the gradient of $r_t$ with respect to $\theta$ is always zero. The policy cannot learn at all — the update is killed. $\varepsilon \to \infty$ disables clipping entirely, recovering the unclipped policy gradient (REINFORCE with baseline), which is unstable for large policy updates. The optimal $\varepsilon$ balances learning speed (larger $\varepsilon$ allows bigger steps) against stability (smaller $\varepsilon$ is safer). The value 0.2 is robust across a wide range of tasks, but in RLHF with large language models, smaller values (0.1–0.15) are sometimes used to be more conservative.
</details>

## 10 · Common misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|---|---|
| "PPO is off-policy because it does multiple mini-epochs on the same batch." | PPO is classified as on-policy. The multiple mini-epochs are bounded by the clip to stay close to the generating policy. No long-lived replay buffer is used. |
| "Off-policy means using a completely different policy to collect data." | Off-policy means data from *any* version of a past policy can be reused. DQN's replay buffer contains transitions from the policy as it was days of training ago — that counts. |
| "On-policy algorithms are always safer than off-policy." | PPO is stable partly because of the clip, not purely because it is on-policy. A poorly tuned REINFORCE (also on-policy) can collapse just as badly as a poorly tuned off-policy algorithm. |
| "The clip in PPO is a trust-region constraint." | The clip is a differentiable *approximation* of a trust region — it removes the gradient outside $[1-\varepsilon, 1+\varepsilon]$ but does not enforce $\text{KL} \leq \delta$ exactly. TRPO enforces a hard KL constraint. |
| "Importance sampling makes off-policy unbiased." | Importance sampling corrects for distribution mismatch in expectation, but when $\pi_\theta$ and $\pi_b$ diverge, importance weights have high variance and the corrected estimator can be practically useless (infinite variance). |
| "PPO is the only choice for RLHF." | PPO is the most common choice. Alternatives include REINFORCE (simpler, used in some SFT-flavored setups), GRPO (DeepSeek-R1, eliminates value function), and offline methods like DPO. |

## 11 · Connections to other concepts

**KL penalty in RLHF (Q4-04).** In RLHF, the reward signal is augmented as:

$$r'_t = r^\text{RM}(s_t, a_t) - \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)}{\pi_\text{ref}(a_t \mid s_t)}$$

This per-step KL penalty acts as an additional regulariser on top of PPO's clip mechanism — two complementary forces preventing the policy from straying too far from the reference model.

**GAE and advantage estimation (Q4-16).** PPO's stability depends heavily on accurate advantage estimates. Generalised Advantage Estimation (GAE, Schulman et al. 2015b) provides a bias-variance tradeoff via $\lambda \in [0,1]$: $\lambda=0$ is TD(0) (low variance, high bias), $\lambda=1$ is Monte Carlo (high variance, low bias). RLHF implementations typically use $\lambda \approx 0.95$.

**GRPO vs PPO (Q4-23).** DeepSeek-R1 uses Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), which eliminates the value function entirely. Instead of estimating $\hat{A}_t$ from a separate critic network, GRPO uses relative performance within a group of sampled completions as the advantage signal. This simplifies the implementation (no critic training) at the cost of higher variance in the advantage estimate.

**DPO as off-policy alternative (Q4-09).** Direct Preference Optimization reformulates RLHF as a supervised learning problem over preference pairs, eliminating online RL entirely. It is implicitly off-policy in the sense that it uses fixed, pre-collected preference data. The tradeoff: DPO is simpler and more stable but may underperform online PPO when the policy distribution has shifted far from the data distribution (distribution shift problem in offline learning).

**Reward hacking and on-policy dynamics (Q4-11).** On-policy collection is relevant to reward hacking: the policy continuously generates new data under its current behavior, meaning it actively explores the reward model's weaknesses. Off-policy methods that replay old data may be slower to find and exploit reward model vulnerabilities — but they also recover more slowly from discovered hacks.

## 12 · One-screen summary

| Concept | On-Policy | Off-Policy |
|---|---|---|
| **Data source** | Current policy $\pi_\theta$ only | Any past policy (replay buffer) |
| **After update** | Data discarded | Data kept in buffer |
| **Importance weighting** | Not needed (data matches policy) | Required (distribution mismatch) |
| **Sample efficiency** | Low | High |
| **Stability** | High | Lower (requires careful tuning) |
| **Key algorithms** | PPO, A2C, TRPO, REINFORCE | DQN, SAC, TD3, DDPG |

**PPO clipped objective:**

$$\mathcal{L}^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[\min\!\left(r_t(\theta)\,\hat{A}_t,\;\text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1\!-\!\varepsilon, 1\!+\!\varepsilon)\,\hat{A}_t\right)\right]$$

$$r_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}(a_t \mid s_t)}, \quad \varepsilon = 0.2\text{ (typical)}$$

**PPO is on-policy** — collects rollouts from the current policy, applies $K=4$–16 mini-epochs with clip-bounded reuse, then discards the batch.

**Why PPO dominates RLHF:**
1. Clip prevents catastrophic policy degradation (stability).
2. Works for discrete high-dimensional action spaces (token generation).
3. Integrates cleanly with the KL penalty on the RLHF reward signal.

---

## 13 · Five-minute refresher

**The core distinction in one sentence:** On-policy uses fresh data from today's policy; off-policy reuses old data from a buffer.

**Memorise the canonical examples:**
- On-policy: PPO, A2C, TRPO
- Off-policy: DQN (replay buffer + Q-learning), SAC (entropy-regularised actor-critic with buffer)

**PPO's position:** On-policy, but with $K$ mini-epochs of reuse within each rollout batch. The clip ratio $r_t = \pi_\theta / \pi_{\theta_\text{old}} \in [1-\varepsilon, 1+\varepsilon]$ is what bounds how off-policy PPO becomes within a batch.

**The three regions of the PPO objective** (for positive advantage):
- $r < 1-\varepsilon$: clipped — gradient is zero, no further incentive to reduce the action probability.
- $1-\varepsilon \leq r \leq 1+\varepsilon$: unclipped — standard policy gradient, slope = advantage.
- $r > 1+\varepsilon$: clipped — gradient is zero, no incentive to increase the action probability further.

**Why RLHF uses PPO:** stability, discrete action compatibility, natural KL-penalty integration.

**Quick numerical check:** $r_t = 1.3$, $\varepsilon = 0.2$, $\hat{A} = 1.5$ $\Rightarrow$ $\text{clip}(1.3, 0.8, 1.2) = 1.2$ $\Rightarrow$ $\mathcal{L} = \min(1.95, 1.80) = 1.80$.

## 14 · Further reading

1. Schulman, J. et al. — **Proximal Policy Optimization Algorithms**. *arXiv:1707.06347, 2017.* — The PPO paper. Short (11 pages), clearly written. Read sections 1–3 and the MuJoCo results.

2. Schulman, J. et al. — **Trust Region Policy Optimization**. *ICML 2015 / arXiv:1502.05477.* — PPO's predecessor. Understanding TRPO's KL constraint makes PPO's clip approximation meaningful.

3. Ouyang, L. et al. — **Training language models to follow instructions with human feedback** (InstructGPT). *NeurIPS 2022 / arXiv:2203.02155.* — The paper that made PPO+RLHF the standard post-training recipe.

4. Mnih, V. et al. — **Human-level control through deep reinforcement learning** (DQN). *Nature 2015.* — The canonical off-policy deep RL paper. Introduces the replay buffer and target network.

5. Haarnoja, T. et al. — **Soft Actor-Critic: Off-Policy Maximum Entropy Deep Reinforcement Learning with a Stochastic Actor**. *ICML 2018 / arXiv:1801.01290.* — The state-of-the-art off-policy continuous control algorithm; useful contrast to PPO.

6. Sutton, R. S. & Barto, A. G. — **Reinforcement Learning: An Introduction**. *MIT Press, 2nd ed. 2018.* — Chapters 4–7 cover the policy gradient theorem and the on/off-policy distinction from first principles. Available free online at incompleteideas.net.

7. Schulman, J. et al. — **High-Dimensional Continuous Control Using Generalized Advantage Estimation**. *ICLR 2016 / arXiv:1506.02438.* — Introduces GAE ($\lambda$-return) used for advantage estimation in PPO.

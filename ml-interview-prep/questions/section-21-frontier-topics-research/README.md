These are open-ended research questions asked at the frontier level. There are no "right" answers — interviewers use these to assess **depth of thinking, honest self-critique, and calibrated views on the hardest open problems in AI**. Each question should be answered with 3–5 minutes of structured thinking, not a rehearsed script.

## Why these questions matter

Frontier open-ended questions reveal how a candidate **thinks at the limits of current knowledge**. Interviewers are not looking for the correct answer — they're evaluating intellectual honesty, depth of engagement with the literature, and the ability to form and defend calibrated views.

| Theme | Key questions |
|-------|--------------|
| **Research vision** | Q21‑01, Q21‑06, Q21‑07 — open problems, compute agenda, self-critique |
| **Architecture future** | Q21‑02, Q21‑12 — Transformer alternatives, 5-year bet |
| **Reasoning & capability** | Q21‑04, Q21‑17 — genuine reasoning, CoT failure modes |
| **Field critique** | Q21‑14, Q21‑15, Q21‑16 — disagreements, benchmarks, AGI timelines |

---

## How to prepare for these questions

These questions have no answer key. Preparation means:

1. **Pick 3–5 genuine views** you've formed from reading papers, not from copying summaries.
2. **Know the counter-arguments** well enough to steelman them before dismissing them.
3. **Have a recent paper you genuinely liked** (Q21‑05) and one you were skeptical of.
4. **Be honest about uncertainty.** "I don't know, and here's what would help me know" is a better answer than confident wrong.

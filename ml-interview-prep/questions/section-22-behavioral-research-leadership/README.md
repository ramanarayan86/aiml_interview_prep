At senior and staff levels, technical excellence is table stakes. These behavioral questions probe **research vision, cross-functional influence, failure recovery, and the ability to lead in ambiguity**. They are especially common in Google and Anthropic research scientist interviews.

> **Legend** &nbsp; ✅ full answer published &nbsp;·&nbsp; 📝 scaffolded (awaiting write-up)
>
> Use the **STAR format** (Situation → Task → Action → Result) for behavioral answers. Keep each story to 2–3 minutes. Prepare 5–6 versatile "anchor stories" you can adapt to any question.

## Why these questions matter

Behavioral questions are pass/fail gates at senior levels. You can have the best technical answers and still fail if your behavioral answers lack specificity, ownership, or measurable impact.

| Theme | Key questions |
|-------|--------------|
| **Ownership & impact** | Q22‑01, Q22‑06, Q22‑11 — driving projects, prioritization, negative results |
| **Influence & communication** | Q22‑03, Q22‑05, Q22‑07 — disagreement, explaining complexity, upward influence |
| **Failure & learning** | Q22‑04, Q22‑09 — biggest failure, collaboration breakdown |
| **Leadership** | Q22‑12, Q22‑13 — mentoring, change management |

---

## How to prepare

1. **Write out 5–6 anchor stories** that are specific, measurable, and personal (not team-generic).
2. **Each story should have a number** — team size, percentage improvement, timeline, or dollar impact.
3. **Avoid "we"** — own your individual contribution explicitly.
4. **Prepare a negative-result story** (Q22‑11) where you killed a project you loved. It signals scientific maturity.
5. **Practice out loud** — behavioral answers that read well often fall flat without pacing rehearsal.

---

## Reading order suggestions

**Pre-interview preparation:** Q22‑04 (biggest failure) → Q22‑01 (big win) → Q22‑03 (disagreement) → Q22‑11 (negative result) → Q22‑07 (upward influence)

**For Google L5/L6 loops:** Q22‑06 (prioritization) · Q22‑08 (problem finding) · Q22‑13 (change management)
